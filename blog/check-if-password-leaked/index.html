How to Check If Your Password Was Leaked in a Data Breach

Find out if your credentials are in the hands of hackers

Billions of passwords have been exposed in data breaches. Your email and password combinations might be circulating on hacker forums right now. Here's how to check—and what to do about it.

The Scale of the Problem

Some of the largest data breaches in history:

  • Yahoo (2013): 3 billion accounts
  • LinkedIn (2021): 700 million records
  • Facebook (2019): 533 million accounts
  • Collection #1-5 (2019): 2.2 billion unique credentials

If you've used the internet for any length of time, your data has almost certainly been leaked somewhere.

How to Check Your Email

Have I Been Pwned (Recommended)

The gold standard for breach checking, created by security researcher Troy Hunt.

  1. Go to haveibeenpwned.com
  2. Enter your email address
  3. See which breaches exposed your data

Privacy: Your email is never stored—only a partial hash is checked against the database.

Google Password Checkup

If you use Chrome's password manager:

  1. Go to passwords.google.com
  2. Click "Go to Password Checkup"
  3. Review compromised, reused, and weak passwords

Password Manager Breach Monitoring

Many password managers include breach monitoring:

  • 1Password: Watchtower feature
  • Bitwarden: Data breach reports
  • Dashlane: Dark web monitoring
  • LastPass: Security dashboard

How to Check a Specific Password

You can check if a password (not tied to your email) has appeared in any breach:

Pwned Passwords

  1. Go to haveibeenpwned.com/Passwords
  2. Enter the password you want to check
  3. See how many times it's appeared in breaches

Privacy: Your password is never sent to the server. It uses a clever k-anonymity model—only the first 5 characters of the hash are sent, and you check locally.

Important Safety Note

Only use trusted services to check passwords. Never enter your actual passwords on random websites claiming to check breaches—they could be phishing sites stealing your credentials.

What to Do If You're Breached

Immediate Actions

  1. Change the password on the breached site immediately
  2. Change it everywhere else you used the same password
  3. Enable 2FA on the affected account
  4. Check for unauthorized access in account activity logs

If Financial Data Was Exposed

  • Monitor your credit reports
  • Consider a credit freeze
  • Watch for suspicious transactions
  • Be alert for phishing attempts using your leaked info

Long-Term Prevention

  • Use a password manager with unique passwords everywhere
  • Enable 2FA on all important accounts
  • Sign up for breach notifications (Have I Been Pwned offers this)
  • Regularly audit your passwords for strength and uniqueness

Understanding Breach Reports

When you check your email on Have I Been Pwned, you'll see what data was exposed:

Email addresses

Common in almost all breaches. Expect more spam and phishing attempts.

Passwords

Sometimes plaintext, sometimes hashed. Change immediately regardless.

Names and addresses

Enables more targeted phishing and identity theft.

Phone numbers

Expect spam calls and SMS phishing (smishing).

IP addresses

Reveals approximate location; low risk on its own.

Social security numbers

High risk for identity theft. Consider credit monitoring.

Why Unique Passwords Matter

If you use the same password on multiple sites, one breach compromises everything. When Site A gets breached, attackers immediately try those credentials on:

  • Email providers (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo)
  • Social media (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter)
  • Financial sites (banks, PayPal, Venmo)
  • Shopping sites (Amazon, eBay)

This is called credential stuffing, and it works because most people reuse passwords.

Proactive Monitoring

Set Up Breach Alerts

Get notified when your email appears in new breaches:

  • Have I Been Pwned: Free email notifications
  • Firefox Monitor: Mozilla's breach alert service
  • Password manager alerts: Most premium tiers include this

Regular Security Audits

Monthly habit: Check your password manager's security report for:

  • Weak passwords that need upgrading
  • Reused passwords across sites
  • Old passwords that haven't been changed
  • Accounts without 2FA enabled

Summary

Data breaches are inevitable, but their impact doesn't have to be. Check your email and passwords against known breaches, use unique passwords everywhere, enable 2FA, and set up breach notifications. When (not if) you're breached, you'll be ready.
