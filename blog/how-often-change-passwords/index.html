How Often Should You Change Your Passwords? The 2025 Answer

Spoiler: The 90-day rule is dead

For decades, security experts recommended changing passwords every 30-90 days. That advice has been officially retired. Here's what the research says you should do instead.

The Old Advice (And Why It's Wrong)

The 90-day password rotation rule made sense in theory: if an attacker steals your password, regularly changing it limits how long they have access.

In practice, research found that mandatory rotation:

  • Leads to weaker passwords: Users choose simpler passwords they can remember through frequent changes
  • Creates predictable patterns: Password1 → Password2 → Password3 (attackers know this)
  • Increases write-down behavior: Constantly changing passwords encourages insecure storage
  • Causes "password fatigue": Users become less security-conscious overall

"If a password has never been stolen, there's no reason to change it. And if it HAS been stolen, you should change it immediately—not wait for the quarterly rotation."

— Security researchers at Carnegie Mellon University

What NIST Now Recommends

In 2017, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) updated their Digital Identity Guidelines to explicitly recommend against mandatory periodic password changes.

The new guidance:

  • Don't require periodic password changes
  • DO require password changes when there's evidence of compromise
  • Focus on password strength, not rotation frequency
  • Allow (and encourage) long passwords and passphrases
  • Screen new passwords against breach databases

When You SHOULD Change a Password

Change your password immediately if:

1. The service was breached

If you receive notification of a data breach, change that password immediately—and any other accounts where you (shamefully) reused it.

2. You notice suspicious activity

Unrecognized logins, password reset emails you didn't request, or unfamiliar account changes mean you should change your password now.

3. You shared it with someone

Once you've shared access with an ex, former roommate, or former employee, change it when the sharing relationship ends.

4. You entered it on a suspicious site

If you realized too late that you may have fallen for a phishing attempt, change the password immediately.

5. You used it on an untrusted device

Public computers, hotel business centers, or borrowed devices could have keyloggers. Change passwords used on them.

6. It appears in a breach database

Check Have I Been Pwned's password checker. If your password appears, change it everywhere you've used it.

7. It's weak by modern standards

Old passwords that seemed fine years ago may now be crackable. If it's under 12 characters or uses dictionary words, upgrade it.

The Better Investment: Strong, Unique Passwords

Instead of spending energy on rotation, focus on:

Use a password manager

Generate and store unique 16+ character passwords for every account. You'll never need to remember them.

Enable 2FA everywhere

Two-factor authentication means a leaked password alone isn't enough. Prioritize email and financial accounts.

Monitor for breaches

Sign up for breach notifications. When (not if) your email appears in a breach, you'll know to act.

Audit regularly

Quarterly, review your password manager for weak, reused, or potentially compromised passwords.

Exceptions: When Rotation Still Makes Sense

Some specific scenarios still warrant regular changes:

  • Shared accounts: Team credentials should rotate when members leave
  • High-security environments: Government, defense, or systems with classified data
  • Root/admin access: Infrastructure credentials with catastrophic breach potential
  • Known attacker interest: If you're a specific target (journalist, activist, executive)

For most personal accounts? Rotation isn't necessary if passwords are strong and unique.

What About Work Policies?

Many companies still enforce 90-day rotation despite NIST guidance. If you're stuck with this policy:

  • Don't just increment numbers (they're checking for that now)
  • Use your password manager to generate completely new passwords
  • Push back through IT—show them the NIST guidelines
  • At minimum, use strong passwords even if you change them often

Summary: The Modern Approach

Don't Change Passwords:

  • On a fixed schedule (30/60/90 days)
  • Just because time has passed
  • If they're already strong and unique

Do Change Passwords:

  • Immediately after any suspected compromise
  • When a service announces a breach
  • After sharing access that has ended
  • If they're weak by current standards
  • If they appear in breach databases

The goal is security, not ceremony. A strong, unique password that you've used for 3 years is more secure than a weak password you changed last week.
