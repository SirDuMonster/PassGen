10 Common Password Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

January 8, 2025 7 min read

Most data breaches don't happen because of sophisticated hacking techniques—they happen because of simple password mistakes that are easily avoidable. Let's look at the ten most common password mistakes and how to fix them.

1. Using the Same Password Everywhere

This is the number one password mistake. When you use the same password across multiple accounts, a single data breach exposes all of them. Attackers know this and routinely test stolen credentials across popular websites—a technique called "credential stuffing."

The Risk

If your Netflix password is stolen and you use it for your email, bank, and social media, all those accounts are now compromised.

The Fix: Use a unique password for every account. A password generator makes this easy.

2. Making Passwords Too Short

Eight characters used to be considered acceptable. Today, it's dangerously short. Modern computers can crack an 8-character password in minutes to hours, depending on complexity.

The Fix: Use at least 12 characters, ideally 16 or more. Length is the single biggest factor in password strength.

3. Using Personal Information

Your dog's name, your birthday, your street address, your anniversary—all of this information is either public or easily discoverable. Attackers research their targets, and personal info is the first thing they try.

Common personal info used in passwords:

  • Pet names
  • Birthdates (yours or family members')
  • Street names or addresses
  • Favorite sports teams
  • Children's or partner's names
  • School names

The Fix: Use randomly generated passwords that have no connection to you personally.

4. Using Dictionary Words

"Sunshine," "football," "princess"—these feel personal, but they're among the first words attackers try. Dictionary attacks test thousands of common words and phrases in seconds.

The Fix: If you prefer word-based passwords, use a random passphrase with 4+ unrelated words, or add random characters to break up dictionary patterns.

5. Simple Substitutions

Think "p@ssw0rd" is clever? Attackers do too—that's why their tools automatically test common substitutions like:

  • @ for a
  • 0 for o
  • 1 for i or l
  • 3 for e
  • $ for s

These substitutions add virtually no security because everyone uses them.

The Fix: Don't rely on substitutions alone. Use truly random passwords or add complexity in unpredictable ways.

6. Keyboard Patterns

"qwerty," "123456," "qazwsx," "1qaz2wsx"—these keyboard walk patterns are among the most common passwords found in data breaches. They feel random but they're actually highly predictable.

The Fix: Avoid any pattern that follows the keyboard layout. Use a password generator for true randomness.

7. Adding Numbers Predictably

Most people who add numbers to their passwords do it in predictable ways:

  • Adding "1" or "123" at the end
  • Using birth years (1985, 1990, 2000)
  • Using current or recent years (2024, 2025)
  • Sequential numbers (12345)

The Fix: If you add numbers, make them random and place them unpredictably within the password—not just at the end.

8. Never Changing Compromised Passwords

When a service you use gets breached, you need to change that password immediately—and any other password that's the same or similar. Many people ignore breach notifications or assume "it won't happen to me."

Pro Tip

Use a service like Have I Been Pwned to check if your email has appeared in known data breaches.

The Fix: Take breach notifications seriously. Change the compromised password and any accounts where you used similar passwords.

9. Writing Passwords on Sticky Notes

A password on a sticky note next to your computer is visible to anyone who walks by—coworkers, visitors, cleaning staff, or anyone who breaks into your home or office.

The Fix: Use a password manager. If you must write something down, keep it in a locked drawer or safe—and never label it "passwords."

10. Sharing Passwords Insecurely

Emailing passwords, texting them, or sharing them in chat apps creates a permanent record that can be accessed if any of those accounts are compromised.

The Fix: Use password manager sharing features, or split credentials across different channels (username via email, password via text). Better yet, use services that allow sharing access without sharing the actual password.

Bonus: Not Using Two-Factor Authentication

Even the best password can be stolen through phishing or a data breach. Two-factor authentication (2FA) provides a crucial second layer of protection.

The Fix: Enable 2FA on every account that offers it, especially email, banking, and social media accounts.

How to Fix Your Passwords Today

  1. Check if any of your emails have been in data breaches
  2. Change passwords for any compromised accounts using our password generator
  3. Set up a password manager to store your new unique passwords
  4. Gradually replace weak passwords across all your accounts
  5. Enable 2FA on your most important accounts
