7 Password Security Myths Debunked: What Really Keeps You Safe

Outdated advice that might be hurting your security

Password advice has evolved significantly, but outdated recommendations persist. Some "best practices" from 10 years ago are now known to be counterproductive. Let's separate myth from reality.

MYTH #1

You Should Change Passwords Every 90 Days

The Myth

Regularly rotating passwords prevents attackers from using stolen credentials.

The Reality

Forced rotation actually reduces security. Studies show that when required to change passwords frequently, users:

  • Choose weaker passwords (easier to remember new ones)
  • Use predictable patterns (Password1 → Password2 → Password3)
  • Write passwords down more often

NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) reversed their guidance in 2017, now recommending against mandatory rotation.

What to Do Instead

Use strong, unique passwords and only change them when there's evidence of compromise. Focus on password quality, not rotation frequency.

MYTH #2

Complex Passwords (P@ssw0rd!) Are Secure

The Myth

Substituting letters with symbols (a→@, s→$, o→0) makes passwords harder to crack.

The Reality

These substitutions are so well-known that every password cracker tests them automatically. "P@ssw0rd!" is barely more secure than "password"—it's one of the first variations attackers try.

What to Do Instead

Use true randomness. A password generator creates unpredictable combinations that can't be guessed through common patterns.

MYTH #3

Longer Is Always Better

The Myth

Just make your password long enough and it's secure.

The Reality

Length matters, but not without randomness. "ilovemydogfluffysomuch" is 22 characters but easily cracked with dictionary attacks. Meanwhile, "Kj9#mP2x" (8 random characters) is harder to crack despite being shorter.

What to Do Instead

Combine length WITH randomness. A 16-character random password or a 5-6 word random passphrase provides excellent security.

MYTH #4

Password Managers Are Risky—Single Point of Failure

The Myth

Putting all your passwords in one place means one breach compromises everything.

The Reality

Without a password manager, most people reuse passwords—which IS a single point of failure, but worse because any breach affects multiple accounts.

Password managers use strong encryption (AES-256, zero-knowledge architecture). The actual risk of compromise is far lower than the risk of password reuse.

What to Do Instead

Use a reputable password manager with a strong master password and 2FA. The security benefits far outweigh the theoretical risk.

MYTH #5

Security Questions Add Protection

The Myth

"What's your mother's maiden name?" adds another layer of security.

The Reality

Security questions are often the weakest link. Answers are:

  • Publicly available (social media, public records)
  • Shared among family members
  • Guessable (limited options for favorite color, first pet)

Many high-profile hacks exploited security questions, including the 2014 celebrity iCloud breach.

What to Do Instead

Treat security questions as additional passwords. Generate random answers and store them in your password manager. "Mother's maiden name: 7x$kL9mN#"

MYTH #6

8 Characters Is Enough

The Myth

Many sites require "minimum 8 characters" so that must be secure.

The Reality

With modern GPUs, an 8-character password using all character types can be cracked in hours to days. The "8 character minimum" dates from when cracking was much slower.

8 charactersHours to days
12 charactersMonths to centuries
16 charactersMillions of years

What to Do Instead

Use at least 12 characters, preferably 16+. Site minimums are just that—minimums, not recommendations.

MYTH #7

SMS 2FA Is Highly Secure

The Myth

Two-factor authentication via text message fully protects your account.

The Reality

SMS 2FA is better than no 2FA, but it has known vulnerabilities:

  • SIM swapping: Attackers convince carriers to transfer your number
  • SS7 vulnerabilities: Telecom protocol flaws allow interception
  • Phone number recycling: Old numbers get reassigned

High-profile individuals have lost cryptocurrency and accounts to SIM swap attacks.

What to Do Instead

Use authenticator apps (Google Authenticator, Authy) or hardware keys (YubiKey) when available. SMS is still better than nothing for low-value accounts.

Summary: What Actually Works

  • Use a password manager for unique passwords everywhere
  • Make passwords long AND random (16+ characters or 5+ words)
  • Only change passwords when there's evidence of breach
  • Use authenticator apps or hardware keys for 2FA
  • Treat security questions as additional passwords
  • Focus on what matters: uniqueness, randomness, and 2FA
