What is a Passphrase?

A passphrase is a password made of multiple random words strung together. The classic example is "correct horse battery staple" from the famous XKCD comic. Passphrases are:

  • Easier to remember: Words are more memorable than random characters
  • Easier to type: No hunting for symbols on unfamiliar keyboards
  • Equally or more secure: Four random words can have more entropy than a short complex password
  • Resistant to attacks: Random word selection defeats dictionary attacks

Frequently Asked Questions

How many words should my passphrase have?

Four words is the minimum for reasonable security (~47 bits entropy). For important accounts, use 5-6 words (~59-70 bits). Each additional word roughly doubles the number of possibilities an attacker must try.

Is a passphrase really secure?

Yes, when generated randomly. A 4-word passphrase from our 3,223-word list has over 100 billion possible combinations. The key is that words must be selected randomly - never create your own phrase using words you choose.

Can attackers just try dictionary words?

Attackers can try combinations of dictionary words, but with 3,223 words and 4+ positions, the combinations become astronomical. It would take billions of years to try all 4-word combinations at 10 billion guesses per second.

Should I add numbers or symbols?

Adding a number increases entropy and helps meet sites that require numbers. For maximum security, adding another word is more effective than adding a single number, but numbers help with compatibility.

When should I use a passphrase vs a password?

Use passphrases for things you need to remember and type frequently: master passwords, computer logins, encryption keys. Use random passwords for accounts where your password manager auto-fills - you don't need to remember them.
